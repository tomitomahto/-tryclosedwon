PART 1
AI Prompts for Interview Prep
Role-specific prep, not generic advice. Surface the right questions before you walk in.
5 prompts
▾
These prompts surface the questions most likely to come up, what each one is actually testing, and the stories you should have ready. Generic prep produces generic answers. Specificity wins.
1
Full prep package for a specific role Use This
Questions, stories, and smart questions to ask — all in one prompt.
Prompt
You are an expert sales interview coach. Generate interview prep for: ROLE: [exact title] at [company] MY BACKGROUND: [2–3 sentences: years in sales, current/last title, notable wins] Provide: 1. TOP 5 QUESTIONS most likely to be asked — with what each question is actually testing 2. TWO STORIES to prepare that work across multiple questions 3. THREE QUESTIONS to ask the interviewer that signal strategic thinking Be specific to [sales manager / AE / SDR]. No generic advice.
The "what it's testing" part is the most valuable
"Tell me about a time you lost a deal" tests self-awareness and process — not the deal itself. Knowing what's being evaluated shapes how you answer. Most prep skips this step entirely.
2
Build a structured story from a real experience Use This
Turn a real career moment into a clean interview answer.
Prompt
Help me build a structured interview story from this experience: [Describe what happened in 3–5 sentences — the situation, what you did, what the outcome was] Questions this story should be able to answer: - Tell me about a time you had to navigate a difficult stakeholder - Describe a deal you're proud of - How do you handle underperformance on your team? Format: 90 seconds max when spoken aloud. Clear situation, clear action I took, clear result with a number. No filler.
Give it the messy version first
Don't clean up the story before you paste it in. The raw version has texture the AI can work with. Polished-before-you-paste usually produces generic output.
3
Research the hiring manager before the call Use This
Use their LinkedIn to anticipate what they'll prioritise.
Prompt
Here is the LinkedIn profile summary for my interviewer: [paste their summary, current title, past 2–3 roles] Job description: [paste JD] Give me: 1. Three things about their background relevant to what they'll prioritise in this hire 2. One or two things they likely value based on their career path 3. A smart opening question that would resonate with someone like them
5 minutes that separates you from 90% of candidates
Walking in with a question specific to the interviewer's background signals preparation. The question suggestion alone is worth running this prompt for every interview.
4
Objection handling — when they push back mid-interview Use This
Prep for the hard questions before they come up.
Prompt
I'm interviewing for [role] at [company]. Based on my background, the most likely concerns they'll raise are: [e.g. "you've never managed a team this size" / "you don't have enterprise experience" / "this is a step down from your current title"] For each concern, give me: 1. How to acknowledge it without being defensive 2. A reframe that works in my favour 3. A specific example or proof point to use My background: [2–3 sentences]
Name the objection before they do
Bringing up your own gap — then reframing it — is more persuasive than waiting for them to find it. It shows self-awareness, which is what they're actually testing.
5
30-60-90 day plan for final rounds Use This
For senior roles where they expect you to come in with a plan.
Prompt
Build a 30-60-90 day plan outline for a [role title] at [company]. Key priorities from the JD: [paste 2–3] What I know about the team: [what you've gathered] My relevant background: [1–2 sentences] Format: three phases, 3–4 priorities each. Write it as if I'm presenting in a final round. Make it specific to [company/sector] — not generic sales onboarding advice.
Name the company's actual product and ICP
A plan that references the company's known GTM motion will always beat a generic framework. The prompt asks for specificity — your context input needs to earn it.
PART 2
AI Prompts for Resume Tailoring
Rewrite your bullets to match a specific JD without fabricating anything.
5 prompts
▾
These prompts rewrite your resume bullets to match a specific job description. The goal isn't to fabricate — it's to speak the same language as the JD so your real experience lands.
6
Tailor existing bullets to a JD Use This
Paste your current bullets and the JD. Gets your language matching theirs.
Prompt
You are a senior sales career coach. Rewrite the resume bullets below to match this job description. Rules: - Lead every bullet with a metric or business outcome, not a verb - Use language from the JD where it fits naturally - Keep each bullet to one sentence - Do not add anything the candidate didn't already claim - No em dashes JOB DESCRIPTION: [paste JD here] MY CURRENT BULLETS: [paste your bullets here]
Why the rules matter
Without "no em dashes" and "one sentence per bullet," the output reads like AI wrote it. The "do not add anything the candidate didn't claim" rule stops hallucinated achievements. Add your own constraints based on what you want to keep.
7
Generate bullets when starting from scratch Use This
When you don't have existing bullets — or you're applying a level up.
Prompt
You are a senior sales career coach. Write 6 strong resume bullets for a [role title] at [company]. My background: - [2–3 sentences: revenue, team size, quota attainment, sector] Requirements from JD: [paste the 3–4 most important requirements] Format: achievement-led, one sentence each, metric in every bullet where plausible.
Give it your real numbers
"$40M CAD annual quota, 110% attainment, 7-person team" gives the model specific material to work with. Vague backgrounds produce vague bullets.
8
Gap analysis — how close is your fit? Use This
Run this before spending time on the application.
Prompt
Compare my resume against this job description. Tell me: 1. Match score (% estimate with brief rationale) 2. Top 3 things that make me a strong fit 3. Top 2 gaps — be direct 4. One or two things in the JD I cannot credibly claim JOB DESCRIPTION: [paste JD] MY RESUME: [paste resume]
Use this to prioritise, not just to decide
A 60% match on a P0 role is worth more of your time than a 90% match on something you don't actually want. Gap analysis across your whole pipeline shapes where you invest prep time.
9
Counter-offer when the number is low Use This
Push back without sounding difficult or desperate.
Prompt
I received an offer for [role] at [company]. The base is [X], OTE is [Y]. Market rate for this role in [city] is [range]. I want to counter at [target]. Write me a counter-offer message. Tone: confident, not desperate, not adversarial. Acknowledge the offer, make the ask clearly, give one brief reason. Under 150 words.
Example Output
Thank you for the offer — I'm genuinely excited about the role and the team. After reviewing the package, I'd like to discuss the base salary. Based on my research and experience leading a team against a $40M CAD quota at 110% attainment, I was targeting a base of $[X]. Is there flexibility to get there? Happy to talk through it if that helps.
One reason, not three
Counter-offers with three justifications sound nervous. One strong specific reason sounds confident. Give the model one reason and it will use it well.
10
LinkedIn connection request to a hiring manager Use This
Short enough for the character limit. Specific enough to get accepted.
Prompt
Write a LinkedIn connection request to [name], [title] at [company]. I applied for [role title] and was [referred by X / found it on LinkedIn]. My relevant background in one line: [e.g. 5 years at Google Canada, led NBS team against $40M CAD quota] Rules: under 300 characters, no em dashes, no "I came across your profile," don't ask for anything beyond the connection.
The character limit forces the right behaviour
300 characters eliminates every filler phrase. The output will be more direct than anything you'd write yourself — which is exactly what gets accepted.
PART 3
AI Prompts for 90-Day Job Search
Run your search like a sales pipeline. Weekly cadence, pipeline management, offer timing.
5 prompts
▾
Most job searches stall because there's no system. These prompts treat your search like a sales motion — with pipeline stages, follow-up cadence, and a clear close strategy.
11
Build a week-by-week search plan Use This
Turn your target timeline into a weekly action plan.
Prompt
I'm in active job search. I have [X weeks] until my target start date. My pipeline right now: - Applications submitted: [#] - Interviews scheduled: [#] - Final rounds: [#] - Offers: [#] Target role: [title], [location], [industry] Build me a week-by-week action plan for the next 4 weeks. Each week: what to focus on, how many new applications to send, what follow-ups to run, and one networking action. Treat this like a sales pipeline — not a motivation plan.
Pipeline thinking beats motivation thinking
Most job search advice is about mindset. This prompt treats it like a numbers game with a close date — which it is. The discipline comes from the plan, not the mood.
12
Diagnose why your pipeline has stalled Use This
When applications are going in but nothing is moving forward.
Prompt
My job search has been active for [X weeks]. Here's what's happening: - Applications sent: [#] - Callbacks / screens: [#] - First round interviews: [#] - Ghosted after interview: [#] Role I'm targeting: [title, seniority, industry] Diagnose where the drop-off is. Be direct. Tell me the most likely reasons and give me 2–3 things to change this week.
Drop-off stage tells you everything
No callbacks = resume or targeting problem. Lots of screens, few firsts = phone manner or role mismatch. Ghosted after rounds = story or close problem. The data tells you where to fix.
13
Referral ask to a former colleague Use This
Ask someone to put your name forward without making it awkward.
Prompt
Write a short message asking [name] to refer me for [role] at [company]. We worked together at [company] for [X years]. Our relationship: [peer / I managed them / they managed me]. The ask: submit my name internally or flag my application to the hiring team. Tone: warm, direct, easy to say yes to. No pressure. Under 100 words.
The relationship context changes everything
A message to a former direct report needs different calibration than one to a peer. Specifying the dynamic produces a tone that fits. "They managed me" is one detail that rewrites the whole message.
14
Follow-up after no response past the timeline Use This
Following up after the deadline they gave you has passed.
Prompt
Write a follow-up email to [name] after my [first / second / final] interview for [role] at [company]. The interview was [X days] ago. They said I'd hear back in [Y days] and I haven't. Tone: confident, not needy. One sentence expressing continued interest, one sentence asking for an update. That's it. No "I hope this finds you well."
Two sentences is all you need
The more you write in a follow-up, the more anxious you sound. The "two sentences, that's it" constraint produces the kind of message that actually gets a reply.
15
Compare two offers side by side Use This
When you have more than one offer and can't just go on gut.
Prompt
I have two job offers. Help me compare them. OFFER A: [role, company, base, OTE, equity/bonus, location, remote policy, start date] OFFER B: [role, company, base, OTE, equity/bonus, location, remote policy, start date] My priorities in order: [e.g. 1. Total comp 2. Career trajectory 3. Location flexibility] Give me: 1. Total comp comparison at quota (100% and 120% attainment) 2. The non-financial factors most likely to matter 12 months in 3. One question per offer I should ask before I decide
Model it at quota, not just base
Two offers can look identical on base and be 40% apart at 110% attainment depending on the commission structure. Always run the numbers at actual expected performance, not OTE ceiling.

I built tryclosedwon.com in one day using Claude. No developer. No agency. The conversations below are real — the exact prompts and replies that shaped every major decision, from the visual direction to the launch call. This is the full play-by-play.

DECISION
Starting point: what actually is this product?
Me
I want to build a job board for sales people but make it actually useful — not just listings. Every job should have AI tools built in. Resume tailoring, interview prep, salary benchmarks. The listings are the hook, the tools are the value.
Claude
That's a strong wedge. Most job boards compete on volume. You're competing on intelligence. The listing is the entry point, the AI layer is why someone comes back. You need three things to be true: the listings feel credible, the tools feel genuinely useful, and the experience feels built for sales people specifically — not generic job seekers. What's the first role category you want to prove it with?
The first prompt sets the ceiling
The more specific your opening brief, the more useful the AI becomes as a co-founder. Vague product ideas produce vague feedback. "Job board but useful" isn't a brief. "AI tools built into every listing for sales roles" is.
🎨
DESIGN
The design pivot message
The one sentence that set the visual direction for the whole product.
Me
The design feels too edgy and dark. I want it to match sites like LinkedIn and Built In. Clean, white, professional. Familiar enough that people know exactly how to use it without thinking.
Claude
Got it. Clean, white, professional. Familiar enough that users trust it immediately, distinctive enough to be memorable. Let me rebuild it.
Name your reference sites
"Too edgy and dark" alone leaves room for interpretation. "Match LinkedIn and Built In" removed the guesswork. When directing design, naming a real product as a benchmark beats any amount of description.

BRAND
The origin story moment
How the brand positioning for Closed Won came from one paragraph of personal context.
Me
I moved to Canada from Nigeria at 17. First job out of university, the company went bankrupt. I Googled "which jobs make good money." Sales came up. I had no idea what I was doing. That's how it started.
Claude
That's not just a personal brand story. That's a movement story. Every first-generation professional, every immigrant, every person who had to figure it out without a safety net will see themselves in that. The brand line writes itself: Closed Won is for everyone who is building a sales career without a roadmap.
Specific facts beat polished narratives
"I Googled 'which jobs make good money'" is more powerful than "I came from humble beginnings." The AI doesn't know which facts are powerful — you do. Give it the raw material and let it find the pattern.
🏗️
DECISION
Choosing the tech stack — without knowing tech
Me
I need to pick a stack. I have some coding background from Lighthouse Labs but I'm not a developer. I want something I can actually maintain. What do I use?
Claude
For someone who needs to move fast and maintain it solo: HTML, CSS, and vanilla JS for the frontend. Supabase for the database and auth — Postgres under the hood, generous free tier, no backend to manage. Netlify for hosting — drag and drop deploy, free SSL, instant. You don't need React or Next.js for v1. Add complexity when the problem demands it, not before.
⚡ The actual stack used
📄Frontend HTML, CSS, vanilla JS — one file per page
🗄️Database Supabase (Postgres) — job listings, waitlist, user data
🚀Hosting Netlify — deployed from a single drag-and-drop
🌐Domain tryclosedwon.com — bought same day, DNS pointed to Netlify
Complexity is a later problem
The instinct is to pick the most impressive stack. The right move is to pick the most maintainable one. v1 doesn't need a backend framework. It needs to be live.
🔧
DECISION
Building the email gate — and what to do with the data
Me
I want to gate the guide behind an email. But I also want to track these people — what stage they're at in their search, whether they want to be on the waitlist, that kind of thing. How do I structure this without overcomplicating it?
Claude
One Supabase table, five columns: email, career_stage, source, created_at, waitlist_opt_in. The career_stage field is your segmentation lever — you can pre-fill it based on how they arrived ("guide-download" vs "job-click") and update it later via email sequences. Don't build the segmentation logic now. Collect the data cleanly and you'll know what to do with it when you have 500 rows.
Schema decisions compound
Getting the data model right on day one is worth the conversation. Adding a column later is easy. Cleaning up a table where everything is shoved into one field is painful.
🚀
LAUNCH
What blocks launch vs what can wait
The question that got the site live the same day it was built.
Me
What do I actually need before I push this live? Be honest about what's blocking versus what I can fix later.
Claude
Blocking: rename the file to index.html, buy the domain, point DNS to Netlify. Not blocking: Stripe, email sequences, full AI tools, 100 job listings. The product works now. Momentum matters more than perfection. Deploy it today, improve it publicly.
The most useful question in any build
"What's blocking vs what can wait" forces a concrete list instead of a vague readiness assessment. Use this framing any time you're stuck between shipping and polishing.

LAUNCH
Writing the launch post
Me
I need a Threads post announcing the launch. But I don't want it to sound like a product announcement. I want it to sound like the beginning of something — like I'm letting people into the process, not selling them a finished thing.
Claude
Then don't announce it. Tell them what day one looks like. Share the number of listings live, what's missing, what you're building next. Invite them into the build, not onto a landing page. "I just pushed the first version live. It has 12 listings, no Stripe, and one AI feature that actually works. More coming. Here's what I'm building next." That's more interesting than a launch headline.
